Understanding the Weber Problem

What is the Weber Problem?

Formulated by Alfred Weber, this classic operations research problem seeks to locate a central facility (e.g., a factory or warehouse) in a 2D plane to minimize the total transportation cost to a set of destination nodes (e.g., clients, retail stores).

The Mathematical Formula

The target is to find the coordinate $(x_F, y_F)$ that minimizes the objective function:

$\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i \cdot \sqrt{(x_F - x_i)^2 + (y_F - y_i)^2}$

Where $w_i$ is the weight (demand) of node $i$, and the square root computes the Euclidean distance from the facility to that node.

Why is it challenging?

Unlike simple geometric center-of-gravity models (which use squared distance and can be solved with a simple average), Euclidean distance minimization does not have a closed-form algebraic solution! We must solve it iteratively.

How is the "Ideal Score" computed?

This game uses Weiszfeld's Algorithm, an iterative optimization procedure that repeatedly computes a weighted average of the nodes, dynamically updated by the inverse of their distance to the current estimate:

$$x^{(k+1)} = \frac{\sum \frac{w_i x_i}{d_i}}{\sum \frac{w_i}{d_i}} \quad, \quad y^{(k+1)} = \frac{\sum \frac{w_i y_i}{d_i}}{\sum \frac{w_i}{d_i}}$$

Where $d_i$ is the current Euclidean distance to node $i$.

Optimization Strategy

Look closely at the connector lines on the grid. Thicker, opaque lines denote highly demanding destination nodes. The facility will naturally get pulled closer to highly demanding nodes to minimize cumulative transport costs.
