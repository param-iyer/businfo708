Mathematical Coffee Optimization

How Does Price Determine Demand?

This simulator models demand as a simple linear function of the coffee price ($p$). When the price goes up, demand drops continuously. If the price goes beyond the point where demand hits zero, demand stays at exactly zero (negative demand isn't possible):

$$\text{Demand} = \max(0, a - b \cdot p)$$

Where $a$ represents the base size of your target audience (maximum market size) and $b$ is the elasticity coefficient representing how price-sensitive your customers are.

Maximizing Revenue vs. Profit vs. Demand

The ideal economic price depends entirely on your operational goals. If we want to find the exact maximums, we use derivatives:

1. Maximizing Demand (Volume Leader):

$$\text{Demand}(p) = \max(0, a - b \cdot p)$$

Since demand is strictly decreasing as price rises, the absolute maximum demand is achieved when the price is completely free: $$p^*_{\text{demand}} = 0$$

2. Maximizing Revenue (No overhead considered):

$$\text{Revenue}(p) = p \cdot (a - b \cdot p) = a \cdot p - b \cdot p^2$$

Take the derivative and set to zero: $$a - 2b \cdot p = 0 \implies p^*_{\text{revenue}} = \frac{a}{2b}$$

3. Maximizing Profit (Accounting for coffee costs, $c$):

$$\text{Profit}(p) = (p - c) \cdot (a - b \cdot p) = -b \cdot p^2 + (a + b \cdot c) \cdot p - a \cdot c$$

Take the derivative and set to zero: $$-2b \cdot p + a + b \cdot c = 0 \implies p^*_{\text{profit}} = \frac{a + b \cdot c}{2b} = \frac{a}{2b} + \frac{c}{2}$$

The Big Takeaway

Notice that the optimal pricing for profit is always exactly half of your unit cost added to the optimal revenue price! As coffee beans ($c$) get more expensive, your optimal price point moves right, and your max achievable margins shrink.
